Fuel planning

Fuel Planning

What is decision point procedure? It is a procedure to reduce the amount of fuel carried on a flight by:
  • ​reducing contingency fuel to only that required from decision point to destination.
When using decision point procedure, you reduce the:
  • ​contingency fuel by adding contingency only from the burnoff between decision point and destination.
If a decision point procedure is applied for flight planning:
  • the Trip Fuel to the destination aerodrome is to be calculated via the decision point.
An operator shall ensure that calculation of usable fuel for an IFR flight with a turbojet aeroplane for which no destination alternate is required includes, taxi fuel, Trip Fuel, contingency fuel and fuel to fly for:
  • ​45 minutes at holding speed at 450 m above aerodrome elevation in standard conditions.
The final reserve fuel for aeroplanes with turbine engines is:
  • ​fuel to fly for 30 minutes at holding speed at 1 500 ft (450 m) above aerodrome elevation in standard conditions.
Following in-flight depressurization, a turbine powered aeroplane is forced to divert to an enroute alternate airfield. If actual flight conditions are as forecast, the minimum quantity of fuel remaining on arrival at the airfield will be:
  • ​at least equivalent to 30 minutes flying time.
A jet aeroplane is to fly from A to B. The minimum final reserve fuel must allow for:
  • 30 minutes hold at 1 500 ft above destination aerodrome elevation, when no alternate is required.
For a public transport aeroplane with reciprocating engines the final reserve should be at least:
  • ​fuel to fly for 45 minutes.
The purpose of the decision point procedure is?
  • To reduce the minimum required fuel and therefore be able to increase the Traffic Load.
Which of the following statements is relevant for forming route portions in integrated range flight planning?
  • ​The distance from take-off up to the top of climb has to be known.
A turbo-jet AC is enroute to an isolated destination. On top of taxi, trip and contingency fuel, what fuel is required?
  • 2 hours at normal cruise consumption.
For flight planning purposes the Landing Mass at alternate is taken as:
  • Zero Fuel Mass plus Final Reserve Fuel.
​A jet aircraft is due to fly to an isolated aerodrome. The aircraft fuel burn in the cruise is 7 500 kg/hr and 5 000 kg/hr in the hold.  What is the absolute minimum additional fuel it should carry?
  • a jet aeroplane we need fuel for 2 hours at normal cruise fuel flow => 2 × 7 500 kg = 15 000 kg of additional fuel.
Why do we normally overlook the descend phase when calculating point of equal time (PET)?
  • ​Because the descend will have an equal effect, whatever destination we decide to proceed to.
From the departure point, the distance to the point of equal time is:
  • ​inversely proportional to the sum of ground speed out and ground speed back.
In a METAR report the abbreviation "NOSIG" means:
  • ​no significant change.

Taxi fuel = fuel expected for taxi to take-off, including any APU fuel;
• Trip fuel = fuel for take-off, climb, cruise, descent, approach and landing at destination aerodrome;
• Contingency fuel = fuel for unexpected deviations = fuel for 5 minutes of holding at 1 500 ft above destination or 5% of trip fuel, whichever is greater;
• Alternate fuel = fuel for missed approach at destination, subsequent climb, cruise to alternate aerodrome, descent, approach and landing at alternate;
• Final reserve fuel = fuel for holding at 1 500 ft above the alternate aerodrome for 30 minutes at holding speed;

Pre-flight Preparation

PET (point of equal time)
Dist = Distance between A and B
GS Out = Groundspeed to destination
GS Home = Groundspeed back home

Dist to PET = (Dist × GS Home) ÷ (GS Out + GS Home)
Time to PET = Distance to PET ÷ GS Out

From the departure point, the distance to the point of equal time is:
  • inversely proportional to the sum of ground speed out and ground speed back.​
​
PSR (Point of Safe Return) 
Endur = Endurance in hrs (flight time to having only reserve fuel available)
GS Out = Ground Speed Out (from A to B)
GS Home = Ground Speed back Home (from PSR back to A)

​Time to PSR = (Endur × GS Home) ÷ (GS Out + GS Home)
​
The Point of Safe Return uses Safe Endurance for being calculated
Stronger winds: a recalculated PNR will move toward the place of departure

033-05 - ICAO Flight Plan (ATS Flight Plan)
When an ATS flight plan is submitted for a flight outside designated ATS routes, points included in item 15 (route) should not normally be at intervals of more than:
  • 30 minutes flying time or 370 km.​
flights outside designated ATS routes
  • 30 min / 200 NM.
Repetitive flight plans (RPLs) shall be used for flights operated regularly on the same day(s) of consecutive weeks and:
  • on at least ten (10) occasions or every day over a period of at least 10 consecutive days.
It is possible, in flight, to:
  • 1) File an IFR flight plan
  • 2) Modify an active IFR or VFR flight plan
  • 3) Cancel an active VFR flight plan
  • 4) Close an active VFR flight plan
