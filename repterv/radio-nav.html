062-01 - Basic Radio Propagation Theory

Wavelength
  • 300/Mhz = m
  • distance travelled by a radio wave during one cycle of transmission.
Radio waves
  • Frequency is the number of cycles in one second in a radio wave expressed in Hertz (Hz).​
First symbol indicates (e.g. A1A)
  • Type of modulation of the main carrier.
Second symbol indicates (e.g. A1A)
  • Nature of signal(s) modulating the main carrier.
Third symbol indicates (e.g. A1A)
  • Nature of the information to be transmitted.
​amplitude modulation
  • The information is added onto the carrier wave by altering the amplitude of the carrier wave.
Phase modulation
pulse modulation
  • used at Radar
transmission
  • Continuous Wave transmission can be either frequency or amplitude modulated.
Single-sideband modulation (SSB)
  • HF Volmet and HF two-way communication.
cycle
  • a complete series of values of a periodical process.
Hertz
  • the number of oscillations per second of an electromagnetic wave.
Keying
  • interrupting the carrier wave to break it into dots and dashes.
Modulation
  • the addition of information onto a radio wave during transmission.
  • Addition of a low frequency signal (tone, voice) onto high frequency carrier wave
Antenna
  • when signal passes through the antenna, the "E" field is parallel to the wire and the "H" field is perpendicular to the wire.
  • an E electrical field and an H magnetic field.
  • A most suitable length of a dipole antenna is ½ of the wave length, the most suitable length of a Marconi antenna is ¼ of the wave length
  • simplest type of antenna dipole antenna which is a wire of length equal to one half of the wavelength.
  • AC will be induced in a wire, parallel to the wire fed with a AC, but remote from it. / the first wire will radiate electromagnetic waves into space and alternating current will be induced in the second wire.
  • slotted antennas virtually eliminate lateral lobes and as a consequence concentrate more energy in the main beam.
  • loop antenna ADF receivers.
  • the flat plate antenna generates less side lobes than the parabolic reflector.
  • horizontally polarized electromagnetic wave has the E field horizontal.
  • to transmit an electromagnetic wave The antenna has to be fed with alternating current of high frequency.
  • GPS satellites use helical antenna because the signal has a circular polarization.
  • polarization Orientation of the plane of oscillation of the electrical component (E) of the electromagnetic wave.​
  • the electrical component of the signal is parallel to the antenna.
directional antennas
  • Loop antenna, parabolic antenna, slotted planar antenna, helical antenna.
primary radar system
  • One directional antenna both for transmitting and for receiving.​
sky waves
  • electromagnetic waves refracted from the E and F layers of the ionosphere
Doppler principle
  • VOR, GPS and MTS and the turbulence mode of AWR
Doppler shift
  • the change in frequency measured at the receiver.
c (in m/s) = F (in Hz) × λ (in m)
Due to Doppler effect an apparent decrease in the transmitted frequency
  • the transmitter moves away from the receiver. MENTOAUTO TAVOLODIK, MELYEBB
Doppler effect apparent increase in the transmitted frequency 
  • the transmitter and the receiver move towards each other.
When a transmitter is moving towards a receiver
  • there is a decrease in apparent wavelength which is dependent on the transmitter velocity.
Attenuation
  • radio signal looses strength as the range from the transmitter increases
Absorption
  • radio wave energy is taken up by the atmosphere
Refraction (fénytörés)
  • the bending of its propagation path as it passes through or over areas of different electrical conductivity.
  • change in direction of a radio wave occurring due to a change in its speed
  • sky waves originate from
Reflection
  • change in the direction of a radio wave occurring as it reaches the boundary between two different media
Fading
  • The interference of the ground wave and the sky wave.
  • at night, due to the combination of the sky and ground waves.
  • from the beginning of evening twilight until the end of the morning civil twilight.
Diffraction (fényelhajlás)
  • radio wave travel over and around obstacles.
  • passing over obstacles with dimensions close to the wavelength.
  • coastal effect is caused by diffraction of the radio waves
maintain effective HF communications the frequency used at a given range
  • should be decreased at night.
Skip distance
  • range from the transmitter to the first sky wave.
  • higher frequency and higher position of the reflecting ionospheric layer.
Concerning the wave propagation in the ionosphere, we can divide the vertical extent of the ionosphere into 3 layers
  • D, E and F layers and their depth varies with time.​
HF radio waves
  • travel during daytime and night-time as sky waves and the range of the ground wave is very small.
  • Reflection from ionospheric layers
Super position of two radio waves
  • interference
VHF / UHF frequency propagation
  • For both VHF / UHF propagation, the space wave is the only propagation path of practical use. There is no sky wave under normal conditions.

  • BFO position is selected: The BFO circuit imposes a tone onto the carrier wave to make the NDB's ident audible/hear the IDENT of some NDB stations radiating a continuous wave signal./hear the IDENT of NDBs using N0N A1A transmissions.

 062-06 - GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite Systems)

most accurate position
  • NAVSTAR/GPS and GLONASS
NAVSTAR GPS
  • 4 clocks
  • basic elements: control, space and user segments. 
  • satellites Nearly circular nominal orbit 
  • Satellites: 24, orbital planes: 6, orbital height: 20 200 km
  • 6 orbital planes with 4 satellites in each plane.
  • recognise which of the received signals belongs to which satellite: individual Pseudo Random Noise code
  • frequency bands are the L1 and L2: UHF/compensated for Ionospheric delay.
  • L1 L2 The path delay of the signals in the earth atmosphere is proportional to the inverse of the carrier frequency squared.
  • WGS 84, is an ellipsoid.
  • inclination 55°
  • All in View: is tracking more than the required 4 satellites and can instantly replace any lost signal with another already being monitored.
  • UHF
  • distance between a NAVSTAR GPS satellite and receiver is determined by the time taken for the signal to arrive from the satellite multiplied by the speed of light.
  • The nearer a receiver is situated to a D-GPS ground station, the more accurate the position fix.
  • differential technique (D-GPS) Fixed ground stations compute position errors and transmit correction data to a suitable receiver on the aircraft.
  • Multi-path effects not improved by the application of D-GPS
  • The closer the receiver is to a LAAS ground reference station, the more accurate is the aircraft position fix.
  • C/A. only civilian 
  • Multichannel receiver
  • Commercial aviation uses: only the 1 575 MHz carrier wave and one code.
  • Selective Availability It degrades position accuracy by manipulating satellite signals.
  • search the sky procedure that starts after switching on a receiver if there is no stored satellite data available.
  • L1 and L2 NAVSTAR GPS: The higher frequency is used to transmit both the C/A and P codes.
  • The C/A code is the only code available for civilian use. It is transmitted only on L1.
  • errors: Satellite clock; satellite ephemeris; atmospheric propagation.

• En-route mode: full-scale needle deflection = track displacement of 5 NM (1 dot = 1 NM)
