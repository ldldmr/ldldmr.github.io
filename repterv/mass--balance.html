031-03 - Fundamentals of CG Calculations​

The centre of gravity is that (i) on an aircraft through which the total (ii) is considered to act vertically (iii)
  • i) point; ii) mass; iii) downwards
The centre of gravity of a body is that point:
  • through which the sum of the forces of all masses of the body is considered to act.
The weight acts in a direction:
  • parallel to the gravity vector.
The centre of gravity of an aircraft:
  • can be allowed to move between defined limits.
  • the point where all the aircraft mass is considered to be concentrated.
Moment (balance) arms are measured from a specific point to the body station at which the mass is located. That point is known as:
  • the datum.
datum
  • It is the point on the aircraft designated by the manufacturers from which all centre of gravity measurements and calculations are made.
  • Is a fixed vertical plane from which all the arm distances are measured.
  • a reference plane which is chosen by the aircraft manufacturer. Its position is given in the Aircraft Flight or Loading Manual.
  • located: at a convenient point which may not physically be on the aeroplane.
  • a fixed point from which all Balance Arms are measured. It may be located anywhere on the aeroplane's longitudinal axis or on the extensions to that axis.
  • chosen on the longitudinal axis of the aeroplane, but not necessarily between the nose and the tail of the aircraft.
The aircraft datum is a ___ reference point that is defined on or relative to the aircraft about which the ___ of any load locations are known.
  • fixed; arms
A location in the aircraft which is identified by a number designating its distance from the datum is known as:
  • station
A load placed forward of the datum:
  • has a negative arm and therefore generates a negative moment.
If all the moments are positive when calculating mass (weight) and balance, the position of the datum would be at the:
  • nose, or forward of the aircraft.
Balance Arm (BA) is:
  • the distance from the datum to the centre of gravity of a mass.
The position of the centre of gravity can always be determined by:
  • dividing the total moment by the Total Mass. M=FxL => L=M/F
